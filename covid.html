COVID-19




Coronavirus threatens to starve even more people. Experts say that by the end of 2020 the number of people suffering from acute starvation could be 265 million people. Before the pandemic there were 135 people starving from acute starvation but now with the pandemic about 130 million more people could go hungry.

Food Shortage?
Tourism in many countries has decreased drastically due to the virus. At first, tourists feared that they might contract the virus while vacationing; however, as time passed and the number of infected cases increased in numerous countries, people were soon required to undergo quarantine, which meant that they could not leave their houses, let alone travel abroad. Countries whose economies depend heavily on the spending of tourists are suffering, such as the Maldives, Thailand, and Macao. Another consequence of the lack of tourists is the lack of hard currency such as coins and dollar bills.
Many schools are shut down, and over 368 million children are unable to receive the nutritious meals and snacks that schools provide them, which gives struggling families extra pressure to feed their children. The image above displays school closures in countries as of May 14, 2020. Countries in the darkest orange are under country-wide school closures, countries in the lighter orange are under localized school closures, countries in blue are not under any school closures, and those in grey do not have any data.
A collapse in the stock market has caused many countries' economies to grow weaker. Also, the price of many commodities has fallen significantly, as displayed in the graph above. One such commodity is oil, and many countries whose economies depend profits made from exported oil, such as Iran, are largely affected as they have less money to spend on other important products such as food and medicine.
Due to nation-wide lockdowns, many businesses have closed as customers decreased to nearly none, resulting in a large number of people losing their jobs and their source of income. They have barely enough to survive through the day, so they have to sometimes skip meals to save money.
The production and transporting of food has been disrupted by the actions taken by countries to contain the virus. This has slowed the process of distributing food amongst the people. Also, food distribution systems in poorer nations are more labor intensive, so these systems might be more vulnerable to coronavirus.
There is no mass starvation or food shortage yet, but there are problems in the planting, growing, harvesting, and transporting of food products. Poorer countries, especially those that rely heavily on imported food for their food source, will struggle in the near future. Soup kitchens and places where food is distributed have been crowding up with more and more famished people.
